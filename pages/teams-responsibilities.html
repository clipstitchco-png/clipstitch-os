🎯Brand Strategy Open section

Brand Strategy exists to understand the client’s goals, market context, and audience before any work is executed. It provides the thinking that keeps the studio aligned, disciplined, and purposeful from the very beginning.

Why it exists Direction before delivery Clarity for execution

1. Department Overview

This department exists to turn business goals into a clear strategic foundation. It ensures projects begin with alignment, context, and thoughtful direction so execution stays coherent and intentional.

  • If this team did not exist, projects would begin without enough strategic grounding.
  • It helps reduce confusion, rework, and misaligned execution.
  • It is essential because strong work starts with strong understanding.

2. Department Mission

Understand the client’s business and build a clear creative direction before execution begins.

3. Team Structure

Lead Brand Strategist
↓
Strategy Associate

Reports directly to Founder & Creative Director.

4. Roles

Lead Brand Strategist

Owns strategic direction, client alignment, and the quality of the thinking behind each engagement.

Strategy Associate

Supports research, synthesis, positioning work, and internal coordination while strengthening the strategic foundation.

5. Responsibilities

  • Review project briefs and identify core objectives, audience expectations, and business context.
  • Conduct research, gather insight, and convert findings into practical strategic recommendations.
  • Build messaging direction, positioning frameworks, and clear creative guidance.
  • Prepare strategic notes that make subsequent creative and production decisions easier.
  • Coordinate with leadership to ensure the strategy remains realistic and aligned with delivery needs.

6. Daily Workflow

1. IntakeReceive the brief and clarify goals.
2. ResearchGather relevant context and audience insight.
3. DirectionDraft strategic ideas and key messages.
4. ReviewAlign output with leadership before execution.

7. Approval Process

Strategist
↓
Lead Brand Strategist
↓
Founder & Creative Director

8. Department Philosophy

“Think before creating.”

9. Quality Standards

  • Attention to detail in research, framing, and recommendations.
  • Consistency across strategy documents and project decisions.
  • Professionalism in communication and timing.
  • Ownership of follow-through and clarity.

Common mistakes to avoid: vague briefs, late questions, and weak alignment between strategy and execution.

10. Daily Expectations

  • Arrive prepared with project context.
  • Meet deadlines with clear updates.
  • Ask questions early when objectives are unclear.
  • Keep strategy documents organized and easy to review.

11. Communication

Communicate with leadership, production, design, and editing teams. Escalate ambiguities early and provide clear updates when priorities change.

12. Success Metrics

  • Strategic clarity delivered on time.
  • Fewer revisions caused by misunderstanding.
  • Strong alignment between brief and output.

13. Collaboration

Works closely with Production, Brand Design, Video Editing, and Business Development to ensure messaging and execution remain aligned.

14. First Week Guide

  • Read the Team Portal.
  • Meet the department lead.
  • Review one recent project brief.
  • Understand the approval chain.

15. Final Message

Your thinking shapes the quality of everything that follows. Take ownership of your work, stay clear and professional, and help the studio move with confidence from the first idea to the final delivery.

🤝Business Development Open section

Business Development builds meaningful relationships with businesses and turns opportunities into long-term partnerships through clear communication, professionalism, and steady follow-through.

Relationship building Opportunity conversion Client trust

1. Department Overview

This department exists to create commercial momentum by identifying potential clients, understanding their needs, and presenting our value clearly. It keeps the studio connected with the market and ensures opportunities are handled thoughtfully.

  • If this team did not exist, inbound demand would be harder to convert and growth would feel inconsistent.
  • It connects the studio’s capabilities with real business needs.
  • It is important because steady relationships produce stronger projects and more sustainable growth.

2. Department Mission

Build meaningful relationships with businesses and convert opportunities into long-term partnerships.

3. Team Structure

Business Development Executive
↓

Reports to Chief Commercial Officer.

4. Roles

Business Development Executive

Owns outreach, conversations, proposals, and relationship development with prospective clients.

Business Development Associate

Supports research, follow-up, meeting preparation, and internal coordination for new opportunities.

5. Responsibilities

  • Identify potential opportunities and prepare outreach plans that reflect the client’s context.
  • Schedule conversations, lead discovery calls, and gather business needs.
  • Prepare proposals, follow-up notes, and internal summaries for leadership review.
  • Maintain consistent communication during the pursuit and onboarding phases.
  • Support strong client experiences by keeping details organized and timely.

6. Daily Workflow

1. OutreachStart conversations with relevant prospects.
2. DiscoveryLearn needs, goals, and timing.
3. ProposalPrepare materials and next steps.
4. Follow-upStay consistent until the opportunity closes.

7. Approval Process

Business Development Associate
↓
Business Development Executive
↓
Chief Commercial Officer

8. Department Philosophy

“Build trust before asking for business.”

9. Quality Standards

  • Clear, respectful communication at every stage.
  • Professional follow-up and accurate detail keeping.
  • Strong listening and thoughtful preparation.
  • Timely updates and disciplined pipeline management.

Common mistakes to avoid: inconsistent follow-up, unclear proposals, and delayed responses.

10. Daily Expectations

  • Maintain an organized opportunity tracker.
  • Respond to inquiries promptly.
  • Keep relationship notes clear and useful.
  • Share progress with leadership regularly.

11. Communication

Communicate with leadership, strategy, production, and client contacts. Escalate sensitive situations or complex deals early.

12. Success Metrics

  • Meetings booked.
  • Proposal quality.
  • Follow-up consistency.
  • Long-term relationship strength.

13. Collaboration

Works closely with leadership, strategy, production, and design to make sure the commercial conversation matches the studio’s capacity and creative direction.

14. First Week Guide

  • Review the current pipeline.
  • Learn template and follow-up process.
  • Attend a discovery call.
  • Understand what makes a strong proposal.

15. Final Message

Trust, consistency, and professionalism are what turn conversations into lasting partnerships. Bring calm energy to every interaction and treat each opportunity with care.

🎬Production Open section

Production ensures that each project is organized, prepared, and executed smoothly. It keeps the operational side disciplined so creative work can happen without avoidable friction.

Planning and coordination Execution support Delivery discipline

1. Department Overview

This department exists to keep every project moving with structure and clarity. It coordinates the practical side of delivery so shoot plans, timelines, assets, and expectations remain clear.

  • If this team did not exist, projects would be harder to organize and more likely to run into delays.
  • It helps the studio stay reliable, prepared, and consistent.
  • It is important because strong projects depend on excellent execution planning.

2. Department Mission

Capture visuals that communicate the intended message with quality and consistency.

3. Team Structure

Production Team

Reports to Chief Operating Officer.

4. Roles

Production Lead

Coordinates planning, scheduling, and delivery readiness across the project.

Production Support

Handles organization, logistics, readiness checks, and day-to-day project follow-up.

5. Responsibilities

  • Prepare production timelines, shot plans, and internal project checkpoints.
  • Confirm that required assets, approvals, and logistics are ready before work begins.
  • Coordinate with strategy, design, editing, and leadership to reduce misunderstandings.
  • Maintain delivery standards by checking quality, readiness, and completeness.
  • Support fast but thoughtful response when changes affect timing or scope.

6. Daily Workflow

1. PlanningConfirm scope, timing, and requirements.
2. SetupPrepare crew, assets, and logistics.
3. ExecutionRun production with clear updates.
4. ReviewConfirm output against quality expectations.

7. Approval Process

Production Team
↓
Chief Operating Officer
↓
Founder when required

8. Department Philosophy

“Preparation creates better execution.”

9. Quality Standards

  • Prepared execution with fewer avoidable errors.
  • Consistency in scheduling, file handling, and communication.
  • Professional conduct in the field and in internal coordination.
  • Strong ownership of timelines and readiness.

Common mistakes to avoid: missing prep steps, unclear handoff, and poor internal updates.

10. Daily Expectations

  • Arrive prepared and organized.
  • Keep production documents updated.
  • Notify leadership early about blockers.
  • Maintain a calm, professional workflow under pressure.

11. Communication

Communicate with strategy, editing, design, leadership, and external contributors. Escalate changes or delivery concerns quickly and with context.

12. Success Metrics

  • On-time readiness.
  • Fewer production delays.
  • Clear preparation and handoff quality.
  • Delivery consistency.

13. Collaboration

Coordinates with Brand Strategy, Video Editing, Brand Design, and Business Development to make sure the production plan supports the broader project goals.

14. First Week Guide

  • Review the current project calendar.
  • Learn the file and asset naming structure.
  • Attend one production briefing.
  • Understand the handoff points.

15. Final Message

Great work depends on disciplined preparation. Be dependable, stay organized, and help the team create a smooth, professional experience from start to finish.

✂️Video Editing Open section

Video Editing transforms raw footage into focused, compelling stories that support the project’s objectives and maintain a polished final experience.

Story shaping Polish and pacing Workflow discipline

1. Department Overview

This department exists to turn raw material into a coherent, engaging story. It brings rhythm, clarity, and refinement to the final deliverables so the work feels polished and purposeful.

  • If this team did not exist, the final content would feel unfinished and harder to understand.
  • It strengthens storytelling and helps maintain a high standard of presentation.
  • It is important because the final impression often depends on how the pieces are shaped and delivered.

2. Department Mission

Transform raw footage into compelling visual stories that achieve project objectives.

3. Team Structure

Lead Video Editor
↓
Video Editor

Reports to Chief Operating Officer.

4. Roles

Lead Video Editor

Shapes the story, reviews the edit, and ensures consistency with direction and quality standards.

Video Editor

Executes edits, organizes footage, and prepares revisions efficiently and thoughtfully.

5. Responsibilities

  • Review footage and develop a clear structure that supports the intended story.
  • Prepare edits that balance pacing, clarity, and visual quality.
  • Apply revisions based on feedback while maintaining the core objective of the piece.
  • Ensure the final deliverable is polished, organized, and ready for review.
  • Keep media and project files structured so updates are easy to manage.

6. Daily Workflow

1. IntakeReceive footage and brief.
2. AssemblyCreate the first draft structure.
3. ReviewShare drafts for internal feedback.
4. FinalizePolish and prepare the approved version.

7. Approval Process

Video Editor
↓
Lead Video Editor
↓
Chief Operating Officer

8. Department Philosophy

“Every cut should support the story.”

9. Quality Standards

  • Strong storytelling and rhythm.
  • Consistency in tone, pacing, and presentation.
  • Professional delivery and calm revision handling.
  • Responsible file organization and turnaround discipline.

Common mistakes to avoid: over-editing, weak pacing, and unclear revision feedback.

10. Daily Expectations

  • Keep timelines visible and realistic.
  • Maintain organized project versions.
  • Provide clear status updates.
  • Ask for clarification before revising in the wrong direction.

11. Communication

Communicate with production, design, leadership, and other team collaborators. Escalate uncertainty or scope changes quickly so work stays aligned.

12. Success Metrics

  • Editing quality.
  • Turnaround time.
  • Revision count.
  • Storytelling quality.

13. Collaboration

Works closely with Production, Brand Design, and Strategy to turn captured assets into a final story that feels coordinated and intentional.

14. First Week Guide

  • Review recent edits.
  • Understand the revision process.
  • Learn the naming and server structure.
  • Observe one full review cycle.

15. Final Message

Every cut matters because every cut shapes how the audience experiences the work. Be thoughtful, stay attentive, and keep raising the standard of the final result.

🎨Brand Design Open section

Brand Design translates strategy into visual assets that strengthen brand identity, communication, and audience recognition. It is responsible for making the work look clear, cohesive, and professional.

Visual communication Brand consistency Design execution

1. Department Overview

This department exists to turn strategic direction into visual systems and polished assets. It ensures that the work communicates clearly, feels consistent, and supports the client’s brand presence.

  • If this team did not exist, the studio’s output would lack visual cohesion and brand clarity.
  • It helps communicate ideas faster and more effectively.
  • It is important because strong design creates trust and distinction.

2. Department Mission

Create visual assets that strengthen brand identity and communication.

3. Team Structure

Lead Brand Designer
↓
Brand Designer

Reports to Chief Operating Officer.

4. Roles

Lead Brand Designer

Owns final design direction, consistency, and review quality across deliverables.

Brand Designer

Builds assets, applies systems, and prepares polished design work for review and delivery.

5. Responsibilities

  • Create visual concepts that align with project goals and brand direction.
  • Develop and refine assets with clarity, consistency, and strong composition.
  • Maintain brand alignment across layouts, systems, and presentations.
  • Prepare designs that are ready for review and usable in production.
  • Respond to feedback with thoughtful updates that preserve the core intent.

6. Daily Workflow

1. Receive briefUnderstand the project direction.
2. Develop conceptShape the visual approach.
3. RefineAdjust details and align with review feedback.
4. DeliverProvide polished assets for handoff.

7. Approval Process

Brand Designer
↓
Lead Brand Designer
↓
Chief Operating Officer
↓
Founder if required

8. Department Philosophy

“Design should communicate before it decorates.”

9. Quality Standards

  • Visual consistency and thoughtful composition.
  • Clear communication through layout, hierarchy, and detail.
  • Professional presentation and careful revision handling.
  • Strong ownership of deadlines and design readiness.

Common mistakes to avoid: inconsistent styling, weak hierarchy, and late revisions that disrupt workflow.

10. Daily Expectations

  • Keep files organized and clearly named.
  • Check work against the brief before sending.
  • Share progress regularly and clearly.
  • Respond professionally to feedback and changes.

11. Communication

Communicate with strategy, production, editing, and leadership. Escalate uncertainty early and confirm that creative decisions remain aligned to the direction.

12. Success Metrics

  • Visual consistency.
  • Brand alignment.
  • Client satisfaction.
  • Timely and polished delivery.

13. Collaboration

Works in close coordination with Strategy, Production, Editing, and Business Development to ensure the final visual work feels intentional, clear, and commercially strong.

14. First Week Guide

  • Review brand references and systems.
  • Learn the design workflow and approvals.
  • Complete a sample task.
  • Understand file and version structure.

15. Final Message

Design has the power to communicate clearly and create lasting recognition. Take pride in the details, protect consistency, and make every asset feel thoughtful and intentional.
